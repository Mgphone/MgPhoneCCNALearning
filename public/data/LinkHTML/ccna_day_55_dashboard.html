RF Basics, 802.11 Standards & Topologies

The Nature of Wi-Fi

Wireless networks operate very differently than wired networks. The air is a shared medium, making it fundamentally similar to an old Ethernet Hub.

Half-Duplex

Radios cannot transmit and receive at the exact same time on the same frequency. Only one device can talk at a time. If two devices transmit simultaneously, their signals collide and corrupt.

CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance)

Because radios are half-duplex, they cannot "listen" while "speaking." Therefore, they cannot use the wired CSMA/CD (Collision Detection). They must use CSMA/CA (Collision Avoidance).

  • Listen to the airwaves. If busy, wait a random backoff time.
  • If clear, transmit the frame.
  • Wait for an ACK. Since it can't detect a collision, it assumes failure if the AP doesn't explicitly send an Acknowledgment frame back!
📻

Radio Frequency (RF) Bands

Wi-Fi utilizes the unlicensed Industrial, Scientific, and Medical (ISM) radio bands. You must know the trade-offs between the two primary bands.

2.4 GHz Band

  • Pros: Longer range. Better at penetrating solid objects (walls/doors).
  • Cons: Slower speeds. Extremely crowded (Bluetooth, microwaves, baby monitors).
  • Channels: Only 3 non-overlapping channels exist: 1, 6, and 11.

5 GHz Band

  • Pros: Much faster speeds. Much less interference/crowding. 24+ non-overlapping channels.
  • Cons: Shorter range. Does not penetrate solid objects well.
  • Usage: The standard for all modern, high-density enterprise networks.

📜 The 802.11 Standards (Wi-Fi Evolution)

Standard Wi-Fi Name Freq Band Max Speed (Approx) Key Feature
802.11b Legacy 2.4 GHz 11 Mbps The original mainstream standard. Very slow.
802.11a Legacy 5 GHz 54 Mbps Pioneered 5 GHz, but didn't catch on quickly due to cost/range.
802.11g Legacy 2.4 GHz 54 Mbps Made 54Mbps mainstream. Backwards compatible with 'b'.
802.11n Wi-Fi 4 Both (2.4 & 5) 600 Mbps Introduced MIMO (Multiple Input Multiple Output) antennas.
802.11ac Wi-Fi 5 5 GHz only ~6.9 Gbps Wider channels, higher modulation. Gigabit speeds over the air.
802.11ax Wi-Fi 6 Both (2.4 & 5) ~9.6 Gbps Brought back 2.4GHz support. Introduced OFDMA for massive efficiency in crowded areas.

🕸️ WLAN Topologies

Wireless networks are structured using specific Service Sets.

BSS (Basic Service Set)

A single Access Point (AP) and the wireless clients connected to it. It forms a single wireless cell.

BSSID: The unique identifier of a BSS. It is literally the MAC address of the AP's radio interface.

ESS (Extended Service Set)

Multiple BSSs tied together by a wired backend called the DS (Distribution System).

All APs in the ESS broadcast the same network name. This allows a client to roam seamlessly down a hallway from AP to AP without dropping its connection.

SSID

Service Set Identifier: The human-readable name of the wireless network (e.g., "Corporate_WiFi"). It is broadcasted in Beacon frames so clients can find the network.

🪝 Brain Hooks

The "A" in CSMA/CA

Wires use CD (Detection) because they can hear crashes. Radios use CA (Avoidance) because they are half-duplex and deaf while talking. They must wait for an Acknowledgment to know the message arrived.

Non-Overlapping 2.4 Channels

1 - 6 - 11

Memorize this sequence. Using channel 3 interferes with both 1 and 6.

⚡ Quick Quiz (Active Recall)

Click questions to reveal answers.

1. Why must 802.11 wireless networks use CSMA/CA instead of the CSMA/CD mechanism used by wired Ethernet? ▼
Because a wireless radio operates in Half-Duplex mode. It cannot transmit a frame and listen for a collision at the exact same time. It must actively attempt to avoid them and wait for an ACK.
2. When designing a wireless deployment using the 2.4 GHz band in North America, which three channels should you exclusively use to avoid co-channel interference? ▼
Channels 1, 6, and 11.
3. What hardware address specifically acts as the BSSID (Basic Service Set Identifier) for a wireless cell? ▼
The MAC address of the Access Point. (Whereas the SSID is the human-readable text name).
4. Which frequency band does the 802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5) standard operate on? ▼
It operates exclusively on the 5 GHz band. (802.11n and 802.11ax support both 2.4 and 5 GHz).
