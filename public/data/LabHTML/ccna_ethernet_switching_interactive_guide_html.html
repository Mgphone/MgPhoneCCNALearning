Ethernet Switching & ARP

Analyzing Ethernet Switching & ARP

This module explores the fundamental mechanics of local area networks. Discover how switches dynamically build their MAC address tables to forward frames, and how end devices utilize the Address Resolution Protocol (ARP) to map IP addresses to physical MAC addresses.

🧠 Theory & Mechanics

Welcome to the Concept Hub. Here, you will find the theoretical foundation required to understand Ethernet operations. The primary challenge in local networks is bridging the gap between Layer 3 logical addressing (IP) and Layer 2 physical addressing (MAC). The visual data below illustrates the cost of resolving this gap on an empty network.

❓ The Problem

Devices use IP addresses to identify targets, but they must use MAC addresses to build the Ethernet frame for local delivery.

📢 The ARP Solution

When the MAC is unknown, a PC broadcasts an ARP Request (FFFF.FFFF.FFFF). The owner responds with a unicast ARP Reply.

💡 Exam Tip: Switch Logic

Switches populate their tables using the Source MAC Address.

Switches make forwarding decisions based on the Destination MAC Address. If unknown, they flood the frame.

Because the ARP and MAC tables are completely empty, PC1 cannot just send an ICMP Ping immediately. A sequence of 4 distinct messages occurs:

  1. ARP Request (Broadcast): PC1 sends an ARP request.
    Receivers: Because it's a broadcast (FFFF.FFFF.FFFF), SW1 floods it to PC2 and SW2. SW2 floods it to PC3 and PC4. PC2 and PC4 ignore it. PC3 processes it.
  2. ARP Reply (Unicast): PC3 replies with its MAC address.
    Receivers: Sent specifically to PC1's MAC. Received by SW2, forwarded to SW1, and received by PC1.
  3. ICMP Echo Request (Unicast): PC1 now builds the ping frame and sends it.
    Receivers: Received by SW1, forwarded to SW2, received by PC3.
  4. ICMP Echo Reply (Unicast): PC3 replies to the ping.
    Receivers: Received by SW2, forwarded to SW1, received by PC1.
Exam Tip: The very first ping on a fresh network will often drop (timeout) while the ARP process finishes resolving the MAC address. Subsequent pings will succeed.

To verify this in Packet Tracer:

  1. Click the Simulation tab in the bottom right corner.
  2. Open PC1's Desktop -> Command Prompt.
  3. Run ping 192.168.1.3.
  4. Use the Play/Forward button in the Simulation Panel to watch the frames.
  5. You will see the initial ARP broadcast hit every end device, followed by the specific unicast paths of the ARP reply, ICMP request, and ICMP reply.

To ensure the switches learn every MAC address, every PC needs to send a frame so the switch can read its Source MAC.

! On PC1
C:\> ping 192.168.1.2
C:\> ping 192.168.1.3
C:\> ping 192.168.1.4

! On PC2
C:\> ping 192.168.1.4

*Note: Pinging across the switches ensures that both SW1 and SW2 process frames from all PCs, fully populating both of their MAC address tables.*

You must use Privileged EXEC mode on the switches to view the tables.

On SW1:

SW1> enable
SW1# show mac address-table
  • Look for the MAC assigned to Fa0/1 (PC1).
  • Look for the MAC assigned to Fa0/2 (PC2).
  • Note: PC3 and PC4's MAC addresses will both appear on `Gig0/1` (the trunk/uplink).

On SW2:

SW2> enable
SW2# show mac address-table
  • Look for the MAC assigned to Fa0/1 (PC3).
  • Look for the MAC assigned to Fa0/2 (PC4).

This command flushes the dynamically learned entries, essentially resetting the switch's memory.

SW1# clear mac address-table dynamic
SW2# clear mac address-table dynamic
Packet Tracer Limitation: In real Cisco IOS, you can append a specific MAC address or interface to this command to only clear a specific entry (e.g., clear mac address-table dynamic interface Fa0/1). Packet Tracer does not support this granular option; you can only clear the entire dynamic table.
