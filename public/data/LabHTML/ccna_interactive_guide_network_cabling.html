Network Cabling

Connecting Devices & Media Types

Before configuring logical networks, you must understand physical constraints. This module covers selecting the correct Unshielded Twisted Pair (UTP) and Fiber Optic cables to connect various network components when Auto MDI-X is disabled.

Transmission Pins & UTP Cabling

Network devices transmit (Tx) and receive (Rx) data on specific pins. To communicate successfully over copper cabling, a device's transmit pins must align with the other device's receive pins.

PC

Endpoints & Routers

PCs, Servers, and Routers share the same pinout structure. They transmit on Pins 1 & 2 and receive on Pins 3 & 6.

SW

Switches

Switches (and hubs) are designed to be intermediaries. They do the opposite: they transmit on Pins 3 & 6 and receive on Pins 1 & 2.

⚡ Auto MDI-X

Modern Cisco switches support Automatic Medium-Dependent Interface Crossover (Auto MDI-X). This feature automatically detects the required cable connection type and configures the connection appropriately. In this lab, we assume it is disabled to test your fundamental knowledge.

Lab Task Breakdown

Below are the expert solutions to the lab tasks from the video. Check off the steps as you complete them to save your progress! Click on a question to expand its answer.

Cable Required: Copper Straight-Through

The Why: PCs and Servers transmit on pins 1 & 2. Switches receive on pins 1 & 2. Because their pinouts are naturally complementary ("unlike devices"), a straight-through cable properly aligns the transmit pins of one device to the receive pins of the other.

  • PC1 to SW3
  • PC2 to SW4
  • PC3 to SW7
  • Server1 to SW8
Cable Required: Copper Crossover

The Why: "Like devices" use the same pinouts. If you connected two switches with a straight-through cable, the transmit pins (3 & 6) of Switch A would connect directly to the transmit pins (3 & 6) of Switch B, causing a collision/failure. A crossover cable internally crosses the wires so Tx connects to Rx.

  • e.g., SW3 to SW1, SW1 to SW2, SW8 to SW6, etc.
Cable Required: Copper Straight-Through

The Why: A router behaves like a PC when it comes to pinouts (Tx on 1 & 2). Therefore, a router and a switch are "unlike devices" and require a straight-through cable.

Routers connected directly to other routers are "like devices", so a standard straight-through copper cable will fail. The distance dictates the medium:

  • R2 to R1 (50 meters apart)
    Use a Copper Crossover. UTP copper is valid for distances up to 100 meters.
  • R1 to R3 (3 Kilometers apart)
    Use Single-Mode Fiber (SMF). UTP and Multimode fail at this distance. SMF utilizes lasers to push signals for dozens of kilometers.
  • R3 to R4 (250 meters apart)
    Use Multimode Fiber (MMF). It is too far for Copper (100m limit), but well within the range of cheaper Multimode fiber (up to 550m).
